Origins and Founding

The Cave Research Foundation (CRF) grew out of the work of dedicated cave explorers in the Mammoth Cave area of Kentucky in the late 1940s. Their early focus was Floyd Collins Crystal Cave (FCCC), where a 1954 National Speleological Society (NSS) expedition inspired further exploration and documentation. Under the Flint Ridge Reconnaissance, Phil Smith, Roger Brucker, Roger McClure, Bill Austin, and Jack Lehrberger led teams that deepened knowledge of this vast cave system. Their shared success highlighted the need for an organization capable of preserving long-term scientific continuity.

In 1957, these efforts culminated in the formation of the Cave Research Foundation, officially incorporated as a non-profit in Kentucky. From the start, CRF sought to promote exploration and documentation of caves and karst areas, support scientific research, advance conservation, and help interpret caves for the public. These goals remain at the heart of the organization’s mission today.

Expansion and Collaboration

One of CRF's first official acts was to join the National Speleological Society as an Institutional Member, reflecting its commitment to the broader speleological community. As the Foundation matured, its mission expanded beyond Kentucky to support scientific cave work across the United States.

In 1971, the Guadalupe Cave Survey—a research organization active in Carlsbad Caverns National Park, New Mexico—merged with CRF. This partnership created a western branch focused on the caves and karst of the Guadalupe Mountains. Subsequent decades saw additional growth: the Lilburn Cave Project in Sequoia and Kings Canyon National Parks (California) joined in 1976, along with the Fitton Cave Survey in Arkansas, a resource inventory project spanning both National Park Service and U.S. Forest Service lands.

By the 1980s, CRF had formalized new collaborations, including a Missouri Project with the Missouri Speleological Survey and the state’s Department of Natural Resources. Operations also expanded to Guadalupe Mountains National Park and Lava Beds National Monument, supporting inventory, monitoring, and conservation research.

A Continuing Mission

Today, the Cave Research Foundation continues to promote exploration, science, and stewardship of caves and karst landscapes. Much of its work occurs on federal and state lands under a national Memorandum of Understanding with the National Park Service. CRF’s operations remain active across multiple regions, including Mammoth Cave, the Guadalupe Mountains, Sequoia and Kings Canyon, the Buffalo National River, and other significant karst areas.

Through the efforts of volunteers, researchers, and partner organizations, CRF upholds its founders’ original vision: to advance the understanding and protection of the underground world while inspiring new generations of cavers and scientists.
