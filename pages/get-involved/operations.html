Where CRF Operates

Most of Cave Research Foundation's work is conducted through five different operations areas which operate primarily on state and federal lands that contain caves and karst.

Projects are done under a nationwide Memorandum of Understanding with the U.S. Department of the Interior, a Memorandum of Agreement with Bureau of Land Management, or through local working agreements depending on the requirements of each area. There are multiple Operations Areas that focus on different areas of the country and they are listed below.

Eastern Operation

Mammoth Cave

Project Coordinator: Bob Osburn

The Cartographic Program at Mammoth Cave National Park is dedicated to:
  • Gathering Detailed Geographic Data: Collecting precise and comprehensive geographic information from the extensive cave systems within Mammoth Cave National Park.
  • Creating Advanced Maps: Transforming collected data into a variety of high-quality maps, including topographic, thematic, and interpretive cartographic representations.
  • Building a Master Data Archive: Integrating all geographic and cartographic data into a centralized archive system to ensure accessibility and preservation.
  • Additionally, all data and maps are shared with the Division of Resource Management at Mammoth Cave National Park to support conservation, research, and park management efforts.

Small Caves Inventory & Data Management

Project Coordinator: Bill Copeland

This cooperative program between CRF and Mammoth Cave National Park aims to inventory and manage resources in the Park's smaller caves. Key activities include:
  • Cave Identification: Locating and documenting less-extensive caves throughout the Park.
  • Brass Cap Installation & GPS Mapping: Marking and precisely mapping cave locations using Global Positioning System (GPS) equipment.
  • Mapping & Data Collection: Creating detailed maps of the caves and recording data suitable for Geographic Information System (GIS) use.
  • Database Development: Compiling a separate database with general information about each cave for easy reference.

Cumberland Gap National Historical Park

Project Coordinator: Bob Alderson and Charles Finney

The Cave Research Foundation (CRF) conducts studies and surveys on the caves and karst systems within Cumberland Gap National Historical Park, an area steeped in early settler and Civil War history. Key aspects of the project include:
  • Mapping Gap Cave: As the largest cave in the park, Gap Cave is a primary focus of CRFâ€™s mapping efforts. Other caves within the park are also being surveyed and documented.
  • Cultural Resource Documentation: Special attention is given to recording the rich cultural and historical resources found within the caves.

Cave Hollow-Arbogast Cave Survey Project

Project Coordinator: Dave West

Cave Hollow-Arbogast Cave, located in the Monongahela National Forest, is a critical habitat for endangered bat species. This project, conducted in partnership with the United States Forest Service, U.S. Fish and Wildlife, and the West Virginia Department of Natural Resources, focuses on:
  • Cave Mapping: Producing an up-to-date, detailed map of the Cave Hollow-Arbogast system.
  • Data Collection: Gathering supporting data to aid in conservation and habitat management.

Ozark Operation

Missouri Cave Inventory and Data Management

Project Coordinator: Scott House

The Missouri Cave Inventory and Data Management Program focuses on the exploration, documentation, and preservation of cave systems across Missouri. Key activities include:
  • Cave Mapping: Creating detailed maps of caves within the Ozark National Scenic Riverways, Missouri Department of Conservation lands, the Mark Twain National Forest, and privately-owned Pioneer Forest.
  • Resource Surveys: Conducting comprehensive surveys of geological, hydrological, and cultural resources.
  • Biological Inventory: Documenting cave ecosystems and species to support conservation efforts.

Buffalo National River

The Buffalo National River Cave Inventory and Data Management Program focuses on the study and preservation of cave systems within Buffalo National River lands. Key components of the program include:
  • Cave Mapping: Producing detailed maps of caves to aid in understanding their structure and significance.
  • Resource Inventory: Conducting comprehensive surveys of cave features, including geological formations and cultural artifacts.
  • Biological Monitoring: Observing and documenting cave ecosystems and species to support conservation and ecological research.

Sequoia and Kings Operation

Lilburn Cartography Project

Chief Cartographer: Jed Mosenfelder

This project began in 1980 with the goal of enhancing the detail of major cave passages. Since its inception, significant progress has been made:
  • Modern Resurveying: The original 7.8 miles of known cave passage has been completely resurveyed using modern sketching techniques and standards.
  • Expanding the Known Cave: An additional 13.8 miles of passage has been mapped, bringing the total surveyed length to 21.6 miles, making it the longest known cave in California.
  • Biological Inventory: Documenting cave ecosystems and species to support conservation efforts.

Redwood Canyon Sediment Studies

Principal Investigator: Dr. John Tinsley

The Redwood Canyon Sediment Studies focus on understanding sediment dynamics within the karst landscape of Redwood Canyon, California. This research aims to:
  • Measure Sediment Yield & Transport: Analyzing the rates and processes of sediment movement across various karst features.
  • Enhance Karst System Understanding: Studying how sediment interactions contribute to the physical development of the karst landscape.
  • Uncover Regional Natural History: Using sedimentological data to gain insights into the geological and environmental history of the region.

Mineral King Project Area

Project Coordinator: Elaine Scott

Established in 1993, the Mineral King Project focuses on the exploration and documentation of caves within the Mineral King region. Key activities include:
  • Cave Inventory: Identifying and cataloging caves throughout the area.
  • Survey & Mapping: Conducting detailed surveys to document cave structures and features.

Lilburn Restoration Project

Project Coordinator: Bill Frantz

The Lilburn Restoration Project was launched in 1993 to address and mitigate the impact of human activity on Lilburn Cave. While major breakage of formations has been minimal, the cave's muddy passages have led to significant dirt accumulation on delicate formations.
Key restoration efforts include:
  • Cleaning Formations: Removing mud and restoring the natural appearance of cave formations.
  • Trail Flagging: Marking designated paths to minimize further disturbance to sensitive areas.
  • Directional Signage: Installing signs to guide explorers and reduce off-trail impacts.

Northwest Operation

Craters of the Moon Lava Tube Survey

Project Coordinator: Mark Jones

The Craters of the Moon Lava Tube Survey is the newest CRF project, focusing on the extensive volcanic landscape of Craters of the Moon National Monument, Idaho. This long-term initiative aims to:
  • Locate & Document Lava Tubes: Identifying and cataloging lava tubes throughout the monument.
  • Create Detailed Maps: Surveying and mapping significant lava tubes with precision.
  • Conduct Resource Inventories: Documenting biological, geological, and cultural features found within the tubes.

Modoc National Forest Project

Project Coordinator: Bill Broeckel

The Modoc National Forest Cartography and Inventory Project focuses on mapping and documenting lava cave features in the national forest areas immediately adjacent to Lava Beds National Monument (LABE). While geologically identical to caves within LABE, these sites remained outside the monumentâ€™s boundaries due to political decisions in the 1930s that prioritized timber interests. Key objectives of the project include:
  • Cave Mapping & Inventory: Surveying and documenting lava tube systems and volcanic features within Modoc National Forest.
  • Geological Documentation: Studying caves formed by the 12,500-year-old Valentine Cave basaltic andesite flow and the Mammoth Crater basalt flow, which produced many of LABEâ€™s 800+ lava caves.
  • Preservation & Research: Providing accurate data to support conservation, scientific studies, and management of these significant geological sites.

LABE Research & Survey Operations

CRF Operations Leadership:
  • Initiated by: Janet M. Sowers (1988-1989)
  • Successive Managers: Bruce Rogers, Pat Helton
  • Current Operations Manager: John Tinsley (since 2012)

The Lava Beds National Monument (LABE) operation, launched in the late 1980s, is a multidisciplinary research effort involving scientists from across the Western United States. Key areas of study include:
  • Cave Cartography & Inventory: Mapping and documenting the extensive lava tube system.
  • Bat & Invertebrate Biology: Studying cave-dwelling species to understand their ecology and conservation needs.
  • Ice Cave & Dust Monitoring: Observing environmental conditions in specialized cave formations.
  • Lava Flow History Studies: Investigating volcanic processes that shaped the landscape.

LABE Research Center & Ongoing Objectives

Janet M. Sowers also led efforts to fundraise and construct the Lava Beds Research Center, which was dedicated in 2004 and now serves as the hub for CRF operations in the area. Currently, CRF supports LABE by assisting with cave surveys, resource inventories, and lava flow history studies to provide critical data for management and conservation efforts.

Klamath Mountains Project

Project Co-Leaders: Joel Despain, Heather Veerkamp, Niles Lathrop

The CRF Klamath Mountains Project, launched in 2018, is dedicated to cave surveys, ridgewalking, and cave inventory in the Klamath Mountains of northwestern California. This project builds upon decades of exploration and research conducted by the Klamath Mountains Conservation Task Force and the Shasta Area Grotto of the National Speleological Society.
  • Seasonal Exploration:
    • Winter & Spring: Lower-elevation limestone caves in Shasta County (eastern Klamath region).
    • Winter & Fall: Higher-elevation karst features across scattered locations in the Klamath ranges.
  • Cave Survey & Mapping: Dozens of new caves have been discovered and mapped, while many existing cave surveys have been significantly improved.
  • Biological Inventory: Over 75 cave animal sightings have been recorded in the California Cave Life Database.
  • Research & Documentation:
    • A 133-page report detailing surveys of 20+ caves, featuring 100+ photographs and 16 research articles.
    • Development of a GIS database for cave and karst features.
    • Ongoing karst hydrologic inventories to study water flow and geological processes.
    • Collaboration with a graduate geology student focusing on the McCloud Limestone.

Future Work (2025 & Beyond)

  • Expansion of biological inventories to document cave-dwelling species.
  • Increased cave surveys and cartography across the region.
  • Continued hydrologic studies to understand karst water systems.

Miscellaneous Operations

Educational Outreach Program

Caves are a fragile and complex resource. CRF wants to educate today's kids so they are aware of how caves are formed, their importance to humans, how to preserve them, and how to enter them safely. The primary vehicle for this educational program is support for the CaveSim Project that uses a portable simulated cave to provide school-age kids with an immersive "caving" experience that teaches them about caves and cave science in a fun, hands-on way.

USGS National Spacial Data Infrastructure Project

In 1996, a grant was awarded to CRF by the U.S. Geolgoical Survey for the development of tools that contribute to the USGS National Spacial Data Infrastructure. CRF, in cooperation with Mammoth Cave National Park, The American Cave Conservation Association (ACCA), and Kentucky's Barren River Area Development District (BRADD), have developed and proposed a minimal content standard for the collection of cave survey data on federal lands.

China/USA Caves Project and Exchange:

Project Director: Ian Baren

This project promotes the joint exploration, mapping and research of the caves and karst of China and the U.S. The sponsoring entity from China is Guizhou Normal University. Project activities have been focusing on the caves and karst of Guizhou Province. In return for sponsoring CRF/NSS cavers in China, CRF and the NSS sponsor and host 3 to 5 Chinese researchers to the NSS convention. Project trips run every other year with the exchange taking place during intervening years.

GIS Resource Development Program

Project Directors: Aaron Addison & Bernard Szukalski

In 1997, recognizing that GIS technology was rapidly becoming one of the most effective approaches to cave and karst resource management, the Cave Research Foundation established a GIS Resource Development Program. The goal of the program is to assist CRF personnel, federal agency staff, and other researchers access and utilize spatial data, GIS applications, and other software tools for the purpose of cave and karst resource management. A longer-term goal is to use GIS to develop a collective knowledge and support base for cave conservation, protection and management.

Educational Resource Development Program

The purpose of this program is to make information about cave and karst resources more widely available through the development of audiovisual and multimedia educational materials. Hardware and software, utilizing digital video, digital still and computer graphics and animation packages are available to create presentations that can be rendered in standard VHS, CD-ROM, and World Wide Web formats. The program has two primary goals:
1) The development and dissemination of educational resources (primarily multimedia and audiovisual) and,
2) the provision of assistance to federal agencies who want to create interpretive exhibits and displays for the general public. Projects are currently being developed.
